The Answer,
Then and Now.

Thirty years ago, I knew AI was the answer. Back then, AI meant Allen Iverson — the fearless six-foot guard who crossed over giants and played every possession like it was his last. Three decades later, AI means something entirely different. And once again, I find myself believing it's the answer.

By Ryan Thomas Filed Technology, Humanity & Hope Read 6 min

Thirty years ago, I knew AI was the answer. Back then, AI meant Allen Iverson — the fearless six-foot guard who crossed over giants, played every possession like it was his last, and famously reminded us all that practice, well, we're talking about practice.

He was the answer to what felt like an unwinnable game. A kid from Newport News who walked into a rigged system and refused to accept its rules. He didn't have the size. He didn't have the pedigree. He had conviction. He had audacity. And he had a city that believed the underdog could change everything.

Three decades later, AI means something entirely different — Artificial Intelligence — and once again, I find myself believing it's the answer. But this time, the stakes are incomparably higher.

I The Lesson from 1996

More than a game, a philosophy

Iverson didn't just play basketball. He represented a philosophy: that talent and heart could overcome systemic disadvantage. That the game could be played differently. That you could take on a system built to exclude you and reshape it through sheer will and brilliance.

The Sixers drafted him first overall in 1996. Philadelphia embraced him as The Answer — not just to their championship drought, but to something deeper. He was proof that the underdog had a chance. That the rules weren't written in stone.

What I remember most wasn't just the crossovers or the step-back threes. It was the belief. Iverson played like someone who knew something the rest of us didn't. He played like the game could be won. And for a city that had been waiting a long time, that faith was everything.

He played like someone who knew something the rest of us didn't. He played like the game could be won.

II Fast Forward to Now

The real games, the unwinnable odds

We're living in a different moment. The problems we're facing aren't playoff series. They're the ones that have haunted humanity for generations.

Disease that won't quit. Researchers have been chasing cures for decades, but discovery moves at a crawl when a scientist spends half their time on grant paperwork instead of the lab. Climate models that can't keep pace with a changing planet. Education systems where one teacher has forty students and no time to see the one who's falling behind. Farmers in emerging markets with no way to read their soil data. Billions of people separated from medical care, from opportunity, from a voice.

These are the real games. These are the unwinnable-looking odds. And now we have a tool that might actually change the rules.

III Where the Hope Lives

Problems we've been chasing for generations

Medicine & Disease

AI is accelerating healthcare discovery in ways that would have taken decades just five years ago. Researchers can now focus on insight and intuition instead of data processing. The bottleneck shifts from computation to human creativity. That's a crossover moment.

Climate & The Planet

Climate models are becoming real-time decision tools instead of backward-looking reports. Cities can simulate interventions. Farmers can optimize crops for a changing climate. We're moving from despair to agency.

Education & Access

A student in a rural school in Kenya now has access to an AI tutor that responds to their learning style, their pace, their questions. A teacher in an under-resourced classroom can focus on mentorship instead of grading 200 essays. Suddenly, talent isn't constrained by zip code.

Agriculture & Food

Smallholder farmers get instant analysis of soil health, pest risk, and optimal planting. Knowledge that was once locked behind extension services and expensive consultants is now available to anyone with a phone. The playing field shifts.

Language & Inclusion

Real-time translation is no longer science fiction. Someone who speaks Swahili can now access medical research written in Mandarin. Deaf and hard-of-hearing communities have tools that were unimaginable five years ago. Barriers that looked permanent are crumbling.

Scientific Discovery

Researchers in longevity, materials science, and physics are using AI not as a replacement for thinking, but as a partner in it. Problems that would have taken a lifetime now have a shot at being solved in years. The pace of human knowledge is accelerating.

IV What Gives Me Hope

The tool doesn't save us. It lets us save each other.

It's not the technology itself. It's what it unlocks in people.

Better questions instead of just faster answers. More creativity instead of just more compute. Engineers and doctors and teachers and farmers freed up to do what only humans can do — to care, to imagine, to solve for the person in front of them instead of the spreadsheet in front of them.

Iverson didn't win games by being the most talented guy in the gym. He won by making his teammates better. By playing with a kind of joy and defiance that elevated everyone around him. He believed the underdog could win, and that belief became contagious.

Artificial Intelligence is a tool. Like any tool, it can be used to concentrate power or distribute it. To exclude or to include. But in the hands of people who believe — really believe — that eight billion people deserve a shot at their best life, it becomes something more. It becomes the answer.

V Same Two Letters

Different era, same hope

Allen Iverson taught me that conviction matters. That the way you show up, the way you refuse to accept the limits others set for you — that matters. That you can look at a rigged game and say: not on my watch.

Artificial Intelligence is teaching the world the same thing. That the problems we thought were permanent might not be. That the underdog — whether that's a researcher in a lab, a farmer in a village, a student in a classroom without enough resources — the underdog might have a real shot.

It's the same two letters. Different era. Different scale. Same hope.

Thirty years from now, someone will look back at this moment and recognize it as the inflection point. The moment when the answer showed up. Not as a single player with a headband and a crossover — but as a tool that a billion people could use to change the game.

I'm betting on that answer. I'm betting on the people who will use it to care for the sick, to feed the hungry, to teach the forgotten, to give voice to the voiceless.
